2000/2001

Low interest rates in 1998–99 facilitated an increase in start-up companies -

  • dot-com boom (similar to a number of other technology-inspired booms of the past including railroads in the 1840s, automobiles in the early 20th century, radio in the 1920s, television in the 1940s, transistor electronics in the 1950s, computer time-sharing in the 1960s, and home computers and biotechnology in the early 1980s.)

The Internet bubble bursts

  • The NASDAQ hits a record high of 5,048 and then plunges by 78%
  • Investment capital dries up
  • Many dot-com startups went out of business
    • After burning through their venture capital failed to become profitable.
  • Some survived and thrive in the early 21st century.
    • The sites that survived and eventually prospered after the bubble burst had two things in common:
      • a sound business plan
      • a niche in the marketplace that was, if not unique, particularly well-defined and well-served.

Growth

  • Many were companies that began as online retailers blossomed and became highly profitable
  • Growth for blogs
  • Some online entertainment and news outlets eventually became economically self-sufficient.
  • Traditional media outlets
    • Newspaper publishers, broadcasters and cablecasters
    • Found the Web to be a useful and profitable for content distribution
  • America Online buys Time Warner for $16 billion in the biggest merger of all time
  • Jimmy Wales launches Wikipedia in 2001 Users write over 20,000 encyclopedia entries in the first year.
  • Blackberry releases first internet cell phone in the United States.
  • The spread of P2P file sharing across the Internet

Viruses

  • Computer programmers begin designing and circulating ones with greater frequency.
  • “Love Bug” and “Stages”
    • Self-replicating viruses (send themselves to people listed in a computer user's email address book)
2002

New ideas for sharing and exchanging content ad hoc

  • Weblogs and RSS, rapidly gained acceptance

New model for information exchange, primarily featuring:

  • User-generated
  • User-edited websites
  • → Dubbed Web 2.0

The Web 2.0

  • New service-oriented startups catering to a newly democratized Web

Web became easier to query

  • Attained a greater ease of use overall
  • Rapid popularization
    • Friendster.com launches
    • Microsoft launches Xbox Live
      • Online multiplayer gaming service
2003

Apple launches the iTunes Music Store with 200,000 songs at 99¢ each

  • Sells one million songs in its first week
  • It's estimated that Internet users illegally download about 2.6 billion music files each month

New companies launch

  • Skype
  • LinkedIn
  • WordPress
  • MySpace.com
President George W. Bush signs the CAN-SPAM Act into law
  • Establishes the first national standards for the sending of commercial email
  • Helps individuals and businesses control the amount of unsolicited email they receive
2004

Harvard student Mark Zuckerberg launches thefacebook.com

  • 1,200 Harvard students sign up within the first 24 hours
  • Facebook goes on to become the world’s biggest social networking site, with over a billion users worldwide

Google starts trading on the NASDAQ at $85 a share

New companies

  • Social news website Digg launches
  • Mozilla releases Firefox 1.0.
  • Massively multiplayer online role-playing game(MMORPG) World of Warcraft launches
